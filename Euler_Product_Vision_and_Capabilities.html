01 Executive Summary

The Problem

Every student learns differently, yet education has always been delivered in a one-size-fits-all format. Private 1-on-1 tutoring — the gold standard for learning outcomes — is prohibitively expensive and impossible to scale. Existing AI tools like ChatGPT answer questions, but they don't teach. They provide text responses without visual aids, lesson structure, or real-time adaptation.

Our Solution

Euler is an AI-powered tutor that delivers live, personalized teaching sessions — complete with an interactive whiteboard, voice narration, dynamic animations, and real-time assessment. It doesn't just answer; it teaches, exactly the way a great tutor would: it draws diagrams, works through problems step-by-step, checks understanding, adjusts difficulty, and remembers every student across sessions.

Key differentiator: Other AI tools are chatbots that answer questions. Euler is a tutor that runs structured lessons with visual teaching, adapts in real-time, and tracks knowledge over time.

Step 1: Intent Detection

When a student says something like "I have a physics exam on Friday", Euler reads between the lines to understand the real need: exam prep with time pressure. Different from "I'm curious about quantum mechanics" which signals broad exploration. Euler detects seven intent types:

  • Broad learning — routes to course catalog
  • Focused topic — starts a targeted live teaching session
  • Exam pressure — time-constrained session with assessment focus
  • Homework help — scoped assistance on a specific problem
  • Content creation — generates study materials (flashcards, summaries)
  • Video learning — processes a video URL and starts watch-along
  • Material exploration — teaches from student's uploaded BYO content

Step 2: Quick Diagnosis (Triage)

Before teaching, Euler runs a brief 2–4 turn diagnostic conversation to understand:

  • Why the student is here (exam, curiosity, stuck on a problem)
  • What prerequisite knowledge they have or are missing
  • Exactly where their understanding breaks down
  • Their preferred learning style (visual, conceptual, procedural)
  • Their confidence level (anxious, overconfident, neutral)

Step 3: Teaching Plan Generation

A background planning agent generates a structured lesson plan with pre-fetched content. This ensures the tutor can teach without pausing to look up information (low latency).

Plan structure: Each plan has a session objective, learning outcomes, and ordered sections. Each section contains topics with orient → present → check steps, along with pre-fetched formulas, examples, and teaching notes.

Step 4: Live Teaching Loop

Every topic follows a three-step cadence:

  1. Orient — Hook the student's attention, connect to what they already know
  2. Present — Explain the concept with visuals on the board (diagram, animation, simulation)
  3. Check — Verify understanding before moving on

Step 5: Assessment Triggers

Assessment is woven into teaching — not bolted on at the end. Euler triggers checks after every section, after 3–4 concepts, before building on a prerequisite, and when a student seems confident but untested. Question types include MCQ, free-text, agree/disagree, fill-in-blank, spot-the-error, and teach-back.

04 Adaptive Learning Engine

Euler's adaptive engine is what transforms it from a chatbot into a tutor. It continuously reads behavioral signals from the student, maintains a persistent knowledge model, and adjusts teaching in real-time.

Student Profiling

Euler identifies six learner types and adjusts its behavior accordingly:

Learner Type Signals Detected Euler's Response
Fast Learner Answers before explanation finishes, asks about edge cases, gets bored with over-explanation Skips scaffolding, moves to application and analysis quickly
Slow / Careful Learner Asks for repetition, long pauses, tentative responses More visuals, smaller steps, more frequent checks
Pattern Matcher Solves textbook problems quickly but freezes on novel setups, can't explain "why" Stops drills, forces explanation, changes one variable in familiar problems
Conceptual Thinker Asks "but why?" frequently, builds analogies, transfers well Leads with intuition and meaning, formulas second
Anxious / Test-Focused "Is this on the test?", apologizes for wrong answers, shuts down after errors Frames as exploration (not testing), after errors changes format not difficulty
Over-Confident "Already know this" but gets details wrong, defensive when corrected Gives problems requiring the skipped detail, lets mistake reveal itself

Persistent Knowledge State

Euler maintains a per-student knowledge journal that persists across all sessions. This includes:

  • Concept mastery notes — freehand tutor observations like "confuses velocity with acceleration"
  • Misconception tracking — specific wrong mental models the student holds
  • Learning style profile — accumulated observations about how this student learns best
  • Session history — what was taught, what was assessed, what needs revisiting
Cross-session continuity: When a student returns, Euler references past sessions: "Last time we covered derivatives and you had trouble with the chain rule — let's start with a quick warm-up on that before moving forward."

Real-Time Plan Adaptation

The teaching plan adapts incrementally based on student performance:

  • Skip topics the student clearly already masters
  • Insert prerequisite topics when a knowledge gap is detected
  • Expand topics when the student shows curiosity
  • Slow down when the student is struggling
  • Never reset the entire plan — adapt incrementally

05 BYO — Bring Your Own Materials

BYO is one of Euler's most powerful capabilities. Students upload their own study materials — lecture PDFs, videos, notes, problem sets — and Euler teaches directly from them.

What Can Students Upload?

PDF Documents

Textbooks, lecture slides, handouts, syllabi. Euler extracts text, formulas, diagrams, and tables — preserving structure and page references.

Videos (YouTube or Upload)

Paste a YouTube URL or upload a lecture recording. Euler extracts timestamped transcripts and enables Video Follow-Along mode.

Text & Notes

Paste in handwritten notes, markdown documents, or plain text. Euler processes and indexes everything for teaching.

Images & Diagrams

Upload photos of whiteboard notes, textbook diagrams, or hand-drawn sketches. Euler's vision AI describes and indexes them.

  1. Extract — The right tool is chosen per file type: PDF parser for documents, speech-to-text for videos, vision AI for images
  2. Chunk — Content is split into meaningful segments (by section, page, or timestamp) while keeping formulas, code, and tables intact
  3. Classify — Each chunk is labeled by type (explanation, formula, exercise, definition, proof) and tagged with topics
  4. Embed — Semantic vectors are generated so Euler can search by meaning, not just keywords
  5. Ready — Material is available for teaching, with full citations back to the original source

BYO in Action: Three Examples

Example 1 — Student Uploads a Textbook Chapter
A biology student uploads Chapter 12 of their genetics textbook (PDF). Euler extracts 45 chunks covering Mendelian inheritance, Punnett squares, and linked genes. The student says "Teach me about dihybrid crosses." Euler pulls the relevant chunks from their own textbook, builds a teaching plan around that content, and teaches using the exact terminology and examples from their professor's assigned reading — drawing Punnett squares on the board and checking understanding along the way.
Example 2 — Student Pastes a YouTube Link
A physics student pastes a link to a 45-minute MIT OpenCourseWare lecture on electromagnetism. Euler extracts the full timestamped transcript. The student watches the video embedded in Euler's interface. At 14:32, the professor explains Gauss's Law and the student pauses to ask "Wait, why does the flux through a closed surface equal the enclosed charge?" Euler references the professor's exact words, draws a diagram of the Gaussian surface, explains the concept, and resumes the video.
Example 3 — Student Uploads Problem Sets
A calculus student uploads three past homework PDFs. Euler classifies each problem by topic (limits, derivatives, integrals) and difficulty. The student asks "Help me prepare for my midterm." Euler creates a study plan that prioritizes topics where the student had the most errors in their homework, teaches those concepts with fresh examples, and then gives practice problems similar to the ones they struggled with.

Collections

Students organize their materials into collections — groupings of related content. A collection might be "Organic Chemistry Final Prep" containing a textbook PDF, three lecture videos, and a set of practice problems. Euler can teach from an entire collection, search across collections, or let students browse and manage their uploaded content.

1. Video Processing

The student pastes a YouTube URL (or uploads a video). Euler automatically extracts a timestamped transcript. For YouTube videos, it first tries to pull existing captions (free and fast). If captions aren't available, it downloads the audio and uses speech-to-text AI to generate a high-quality transcript.

2. Watch Experience

The video plays in a floating player within Euler's interface. The student watches normally. Underneath the video, the teaching board is ready for Euler to draw on.

3. Pause-and-Ask

When the student is confused, they simply pause the video and type (or speak) their question. Euler automatically knows the timestamp where they paused and pulls the surrounding transcript (30 seconds before and after) for context.

4. Contextual Explanation

Euler answers by referencing the professor's exact words: "Around 14:30, the professor mentioned that flux depends on the enclosed charge — what this means is..." If needed, Euler draws diagrams on the board, works through the math, or provides an analogy.

5. Automatic Resume

After answering, Euler immediately resumes the video. It doesn't ask follow-up questions — if the student needs more help, they simply pause again. This keeps the flow natural and minimizes interruption to the viewing experience.

Key design principle: Euler supplements the video — it never replaces it. The professor's lecture is the primary content. Euler fills gaps, clarifies confusion, and adds visual aids the video lacks.

07 Interactive Board & Animations

The teaching board is the centerpiece of Euler's visual teaching approach. It's a dynamic, scrollable chalkboard where Euler draws diagrams, writes equations, builds step-by-step solutions, and runs interactive animations — all synchronized with voice narration.

Board Capabilities

Text & Equations

Chalk-style text with character-by-character reveal animation. Full LaTeX math rendering for equations, formulas, and proofs.

Step-by-Step Solutions

Numbered steps that appear sequentially, building up a complete solution. Euler can annotate, strikeout, or update previous steps.

Comparison Tables

Side-by-side two-column comparisons for contrasting concepts, methods, or approaches.

Callouts & Highlights

Color-coded emphasis boxes for key points, warnings, and "remember this" moments. Elements glow when referenced.

Live Animations (p5.js)

Interactive simulations: wave interference, pendulum motion, circuit diagrams, sorting algorithms, molecular structures — rendered inline on the board.

Multi-Scene Flow

As teaching progresses, Euler creates new "scenes" (like slides). Previous scenes are dimmed but remain visible for scrolling back.

Animation System

Euler generates custom interactive animations during teaching. These aren't pre-built — they're created on-the-fly to match the exact concept being taught.

Example — Teaching Wave Interference
Euler draws two sine waves on the board and says: "Watch what happens when these two waves meet." An animation plays showing the waves approaching each other. The student can see constructive interference (waves add up) and destructive interference (waves cancel out) in real-time. Euler then freezes the animation at key moments to annotate the result on the board.

Board Visual Language

The board uses a deliberate color system modeled after classroom chalkboards:

08 Voice Mode & Real-Time Interaction

Voice mode transforms Euler from a text interface into a spoken teaching experience. The tutor narrates while drawing on the board — exactly like a professor giving a lecture, but personalized and interactive.

How Voice Teaching Works

Each teaching response is structured as a series of "beats" — synchronized pairs of narration and board action. Euler says something while simultaneously drawing or animating on the board, creating a natural teaching cadence.

Example — Beat Sequence
Beat 1: Euler says "Let's look at Newton's Second Law" while writing "F = ma" on the board
Beat 2: Euler says "Force equals mass times acceleration" while drawing an arrow diagram
Beat 3: Euler says "So if we double the mass..." while updating the diagram
Beat 4: Euler asks "What happens to the acceleration?" and waits for the student

Real-Time Controls

Interrupt

The student can start typing at any time to interrupt Euler mid-sentence. The tutor stops speaking immediately and reads the student's message.

Pause / Resume

Pause to take notes or think. Audio and board animations freeze. Resume when ready — Euler picks up exactly where it left off.

Speed Control

Adjust playback speed from 0.75x to 2x. Helpful for review (faster) or complex topics (slower).

Instant Stop

One-click stop kills everything: streaming, audio, animations. Resets to idle for a fresh start.

Voice Technology

Euler uses server-side text-to-speech (ElevenLabs) to generate natural-sounding narration. Audio is streamed in real-time over WebSocket as binary frames, tagged with beat numbers for perfect synchronization with board actions. If TTS fails for a beat, the system gracefully skips audio and shows the text on screen instead — teaching never stops.

Why server-side TTS? Previous client-side approaches caused performance issues on student devices and produced inconsistent voice quality. Server-side TTS delivers professional-grade narration with low latency, regardless of the student's hardware.

How Components Interact

  • The Orchestrator (Euler) is the front door. It understands what the student wants and decides what to do: start a teaching session, create materials, process a video, or search content.
  • Sub-agents (Planner, Assessor, Researcher) run in the background, producing lesson plans, generating assessment questions, and finding supplementary content — without adding latency to the teaching flow.
  • The Tutor is the live teacher. It follows the plan, draws on the board, speaks, and adapts based on the student's responses.
  • The Knowledge DB stores everything Euler learns about each student across sessions, enabling continuity and personalization.
