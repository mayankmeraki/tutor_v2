01Executive Summary

Euler is an AI tutoring platform that delivers the experience of a 1-on-1 expert tutor at scale. Unlike traditional AI chatbots that respond with text, Euler draws on a live board, speaks aloud, adapts to each student's level, and assesses understanding through a multi-modal real-time interface that mirrors a human professor at a chalkboard.

Bloom's "2-sigma problem" (1984) showed 1-on-1 tutoring produces students 2 standard deviations above classroom learners. Euler is the first AI platform engineered specifically to replicate that effect — not by mimicking a chatbot, but by replicating the cognitive scaffolding and visual reasoning of a great human tutor.

Every student should have access to a tutor that:

03What is Euler?

Euler is a real-time, multi-modal AI tutor that runs in the browser. The student opens a session, asks a topic ("teach me string theory" or "I'm stuck on chain rule"), and within seconds Euler:

  1. Triages their current understanding through 2-4 calibration questions
  2. Plans a personalized lesson structure (sections, topics, checkpoints)
  3. Teaches using a live chalkboard with synchronized voice narration
  4. Adapts in real time based on engagement signals and student responses
  5. Assesses understanding through inline questions and code exercises
  6. Remembers what worked, what didn't, and what the student knows for next time

The Core Experience

Imagine a top professor at a chalkboard. They write the equation, point at it as they explain, draw a diagram beside it, ask "what happens if we change this term?", wait for your answer, then update the diagram to show the consequence. That's the experience Euler delivers, but personalized to each student and scalable to millions.

07The Live Board

The board is the heart of Euler — a live chalkboard where the tutor draws everything it explains. Unlike slides (static, pre-made) or chat (linear text dump), the board is built incrementally beat-by-beat, with each piece of content synchronized to spoken narration.

What the Tutor Can Draw

Layout Blocks

Smart containers the tutor uses to organize thinking on the board:

BlockWhat it showsUsed For
Split viewEquation or term on the left, meaning on the rightFormulas paired with intuition, code with comments
Process flowA chain A → B → C with glowing nodesBiology pipelines, algorithms, request lifecycles
Diff / compareBefore and after, or side-by-side optionsBug fixes, correcting a misconception, A vs B
Question blockA visually distinct prompt the student must answerReal assessments woven into teaching

Drawing Vocabulary

The tutor picks the right visual for each idea — not a wall of text, and not generic slides. Common visuals include:

  • Headings and titled text for structure and emphasis
  • Rendered math equations with optional side-notes explaining each symbol
  • Callout boxes for takeaways, warnings, and key insights
  • Code snippets in three flavors: read-only, editable worksheet, or fully-runnable
  • Numbered steps, bullet lists, ✓/✗ markers for sequences and checks
  • Animations — 2D diagrams or 3D scenes that render alongside narration
  • Figures that pair an animation with a beat-by-beat narration column
  • Flowcharts, state diagrams, sequences for system-level thinking
  • Arrows between elements to show how ideas connect
  • Edit, strikeout, or erase existing elements as the lesson evolves
Why this matters: Each visual has a cognitive purpose. Split views teach via duality (notation ↔ meaning). Diffs teach via contrast. Flows teach via causality. The tutor picks the right tool for the concept — like a great professor knowing when to draw a diagram, write an equation, or show a comparison.

08Voice Teaching

A lesson unfolds in beats — short units where a new idea is drawn on the board while being spoken aloud. Speech and visuals are locked together so the student sees the exact thing being explained at the moment it's explained.

How Voice Teaching Works

  • Speech complements the board, doesn't narrate it. The tutor says "Here's the equation" and writes it — it doesn't say "I'm now writing the equation."
  • Every beat has real substance. No filler or silent beats — each beat advances the lesson.
  • One idea per beat. Short spoken chunks (~15 words) keep cognitive load low.
  • Attention pointers. When the tutor says "notice this term," that specific element on the board pulses or gets highlighted, so the student's eye follows.
  • Short scenes, frequent check-ins. Every 6–8 beats the tutor asks a question, preventing passive watching.

Cursor and Attention

A visible cursor shows where the tutor is "looking" on the board at any moment — writing new content, tapping a specific element for emphasis, or pulsing to highlight something mentioned in speech. This replicates the way a human teacher's hand gestures direct attention during a lecture.

09Animations

Euler generates production-quality animations on the fly — the kind you'd see in a 3Blue1Brown video or a Brilliant.org interactive — but built live during the lesson, tailored to what's being taught in that moment.

3D Scenes

For anything with spatial structure — molecules, orbitals, fields, waves, surfaces, protein structures, crystal lattices, neural network loss landscapes, planetary motion. Students can drag to rotate and scroll to zoom, turning an abstract concept into something they can inspect from any angle.

2D Diagrams

For arrays, trees, graphs, circuits, function plots, sorting algorithms, flowcharts, geometry. Clean, high-contrast visuals with a consistent design language.

What Makes the Animations Special

  • Phase reveal. Animations build piece-by-piece in sync with narration — students see the idea form, not a finished image.
  • Interactive. Drag to rotate 3D scenes, pause or speed up playback, explore from any angle.
  • Purpose-built. Each animation is generated for this lesson, this concept — not pulled from a stock library.
  • Design consistency. Unified color palette, typography, and visual language across every animation so the platform feels coherent.

10Adaptive Pedagogy Engine

Euler doesn't follow a script. It reads the student's pulse and adapts in real time.

Engagement Detection

SignalTutor's Response
Engaged (long answers, follow-up questions, "oh wait...") Match energy. Push deeper. Introduce edge cases.
Coasting (correct but short, following without thinking) Draw incomplete diagram → "What goes here?" Force their opinion.
Disengaging (one-word: "ok", "sure") Switch to VISUAL: build interactive widget, animation. More text never re-engages.
Lost ("I don't know") Build something concrete they can play with. Never just explain more.
Wrong Build visual that reveals error. Don't correct in text — show why.
Frustrated ("just tell me") Acknowledge briefly. Build interactive immediately. Let them choose what's next.

Bloom's Taxonomy Tracking

Every concept is tracked at one of six levels:

Remember

Recall the definition

Understand

Explain in own words

Apply

Use in familiar problems

Analyze

Adapt to novel setups

Evaluate

Judge when it breaks

Create

Design new solutions

Golden Rule: Always teach one level above where the student currently is. Detect their level by asking one level above — if they handle it, they're at the higher level; if not, drop down.

11Assessment System

Assessments are woven into teaching, not bolted on as separate quiz modules. Seven formats, mixed for variety:

FormatPurposeExample
MCQ Quick recall, common warm-up "Which of these is NOT conserved?"
Free Response Open-ended explanation "Explain why entropy increases"
Fill-in-the-Blank Recall key terms/formulas "E = ___ c²"
Spot the Error Debugging mindset Show flawed proof, find the mistake
Confidence Check Metacognitive calibration Rate own understanding before reveal
Teach-Back Deep comprehension test "Explain this back to me in your words"
Canvas Derivation Active production Student writes proof on board, tutor gives feedback

Question Design Principles

  • Grounded in this course's content — not generic textbook
  • Distractors are weapons — each wrong option targets a specific misconception
  • Mix formats — never repeat the same format twice in a row
  • Anchor every question to the board — speech is transient, text is referenceable
  • Misconception-targeted — ask questions where confused concepts collide

12Code Execution & Interactive Code

For programming topics, Euler offers true interactive code execution — Python runs directly in the student's browser with no server round-trip, so it's instant and scales without infrastructure cost.

Three Code Modes

Read-Only

Static syntax-highlighted code. Used for reference, templates, examples to study.

Editable Worksheet

Student fills in blanks or edits inline. Tutor sees their answer automatically. Used for trace exercises and fill-in practice.

Full Runner

Editor + Run button + test case table. Code executes, pass/fail shown live.

Predict-Verify Pattern

  1. Tutor builds code line-by-line (beats), narrating each piece
  2. Tutor predicts the output BEFORE running ("this should print 49")
  3. Code runs, actual output appears
  4. If they match, move on. If not, debug together in real time.

Tutor Sees the Student's Code

When the student edits or runs code, the tutor automatically sees their current code, which tests passed or failed, and what the failure was — so it can respond to the actual state of the student's work without them needing to copy-paste or re-explain.

13How Euler Stays Fast

From the student's view there's one seamless tutor. Underneath, the platform quietly runs several specialized capabilities in parallel so the student never waits for the system to "think."

Teaching

Generates board content, spoken narration, and questions. The primary student-facing experience.

Lesson Planning (background)

As soon as the session starts, a personalized lesson structure is being drawn up in the background — topics, checkpoints, teaching approach — while teaching begins.

Assessment

Generates questions matched to what was just taught, and evaluates the depth of the student's responses.

Content Retrieval

Pulls relevant sections from curriculum, uploaded materials, or prior lectures for context-grounded teaching.

Research

Web and image search for external knowledge, current examples, and supplementary material beyond the curriculum.

Focused Practice

When a drill is needed, a time-boxed practice session is spun up and handed back to the main tutor when done — so practice stays focused.

Why it matters: The student never waits. Lesson planning happens while the first board is being drawn. Content retrieval, assessment evaluation, and research all run alongside teaching, not in sequence. The system feels instant because work is distributed — not because any single piece is unusually fast.

14Triage Mode (Calibration)

Every session starts with a 2-4 turn diagnostic conversation. Not a test — a warm chat.

Goal: figure out where the student actually is so the session can be precisely targeted. Five dimensions probed:

DimensionWhy it matters
Goal Clarity Exam? Curiosity? Stuck? Foundations? — Determines session structure
Starting Knowledge Ask them to recall/describe — what they say reveals truth (not self-rating)
Boundary of Mastery "You know calculus basics — derivative of x³? Chain rule for sin(x²)?" — finds exact edge
Prerequisites "For eigenvalues you need linear algebra — can you multiply 2×2 matrices?"
Misconceptions "When you hear 'wave function' what picture comes to mind?" — surfaces wrong models

Triage is conversational — each question reacts to the previous answer. Stops when there's enough signal (no fixed quota).

15Session Persistence & Student Model

Euler remembers everything across sessions:

What's Persisted

  • Full transcript of every beat and message
  • Lesson plan state with completed and remaining topics
  • Board snapshots at every point in the session
  • Assessment scores and per-concept mastery
  • Full session context so resumption is seamless

The Student Model

Per-Concept Mastery

For each concept the student has encountered, Euler records what they know, what they don't, any observed misconceptions, their current Bloom's level, and which teaching approach worked for them.

Profile Notes

Cross-cutting patterns: how the student learns best (visual, kinesthetic, conceptual), where they tend to get stuck, what's worked before, and how calibrated their confidence is.

Session Continuity

Resume picks up exactly where you left off. The tutor reads previous context, sees the board state, jumps to the next planned topic, and carries forward everything it learned about the student.

Auto-Generated Study Materials

  • Flashcards with spaced repetition stats
  • Personalized study plans
  • Revision notes from session content
  • Searchable by concept across all past sessions

17What Makes Euler Unique

Competitive Moat

Not slides, not chat — a real chalkboard. The student watches a lesson unfold visually, synchronized with voice. Replicates an MIT professor at the blackboard.

2. Voice-First Teaching

Speech + visual instead of dump of prose. Student LISTENS while watching the board (low cognitive load) instead of reading walls of text.

8. Persistent Student Memory
