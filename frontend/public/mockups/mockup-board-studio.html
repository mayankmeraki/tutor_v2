Chapter 4 · Electromagnetism

How a transformer works

From "moving magnets make voltage" to the device that powers your laptop.

Magnetic field Flux Faraday's law Transformer geometry Turns ratio

1. The lesson, in one line

A changing magnetic field, on its own, creates a voltage in any loop of wire it passes through. That single sentence is the whole reason your phone charger has two coils inside, why power lines can ship electricity hundreds of miles, and why bicycle dynamos light up your way home.

We'll get to the device. First we need to make the rule itself unforgettable: it's not the magnetic field that induces voltage — it's the rate at which the field is changing. Hold a magnet still inside a coil and nothing happens. Move it, even slowly, and a needle on a connected meter flicks.

Faraday wrote the rule down in 1831:

EMF = − dΦ / dt eq. 1

The minus sign is Lenz's law dressing — the induced voltage opposes the change that caused it. We'll come back to that. For now, take the bigger idea: change in flux causes voltage. No motion, no voltage.

2. Now, two coils sharing a core

A transformer is the simplest device that wrings useful work out of Faraday's rule. Wrap two coils around a shared iron core. Push alternating current through the first one — call it the primary. Because it's AC, the field through the core is constantly reversing. That reversing field threads the secondary coil too. And by Faraday: a changing field through the secondary creates a voltage in it.

Whatever voltage you put on the primary appears on the secondary, scaled by the turns ratio:

Vs / Vp = Ns / Np eq. 2

More turns on the secondary = step-up. Fewer turns = step-down. That's why the brick on your laptop charger is heavy — it's literally a small transformer pressing 120 V down to about 20 V.
