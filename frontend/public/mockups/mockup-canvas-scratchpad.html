A transformer is the simplest device that wrings useful work out of Faraday's rule. Before we put it together, the rule itself needs to land — it's not the magnetic field that matters, it's the rate at which the field is changing.

You · question · 0:31
Wait — why is there a minus sign in front of dΦ/dt? Is it just a sign convention?
Sharp question. The minus sign isn't cosmetic — it's Lenz's law. The induced voltage opposes the change in flux. If you push a magnet in, the current in the loop creates a field that pushes back. We'll come back to this when we look at why the iron core matters.
Euler · sketch · 0:38
Same flux fills both coils. Whatever happens in the primary, happens in the secondary — almost.
You · attempt · 0:48
OK so since both coils share the same flux, the voltage on each should be the same? So Vs = Vp ← that feels wrong though?
You're almost there — the shared flux is right, but the voltage induced in each coil is proportional to its number of turns. Each loop of wire sees the same dΦ/dt, so more loops = more voltage stacked up. That gives you the turns ratio:
Euler · math · 0:51
Vs ⁄ Vp  =  Ns ⁄ Np EQ 2 · turns ratio
Euler · note · 0:53
⚡ Key insight
More turns on the secondary = step-up. Fewer turns = step-down. That's why the brick on your laptop charger is heavy — it's a tiny transformer pressing 120 V down to ~20 V.
You · worked example · 1:02
For my laptop charger: 120 V → 20 V
Ratio = 1/6 → so Ns / Np = 1/6
If primary has 600 turns, secondary has 100 ✓
Exactly right. Real chargers also have some loss + leakage, so a real 120→20 V transformer might use slightly more secondary turns to compensate. We'll quantify that next.
