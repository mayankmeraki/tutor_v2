A changing field, quietly, makes a voltage.

The transformer is the simplest device that wrings useful work out of Faraday's rule — and the reason your laptop charger has two coils inside.

I. The lesson, in one line

It is the rate of change, not the field itself.

Hold a magnet still inside a coil of wire and absolutely nothing happens. The needle on a connected meter sits at zero. Move the same magnet — even slowly — toward or away from the coil, and the needle flicks. That is electromagnetic induction.

The careful sentence to remember is this: a changing magnetic flux through a loop of wire induces a voltage in the loop. The word changing is the whole game. Stationary fields do nothing. Even a very strong but unchanging field does nothing. The induction is in the verb, not the noun.

Faraday wrote this rule down in 1831 in the form we still use:

ℰ = − dΦ / dt eq. 1 · faraday

The minus sign is Lenz's dressing — induced voltage opposes the change that caused it. We will return to that. For now, take only the bigger idea: change in flux creates voltage. No motion, no voltage. Slow motion, weak voltage. Fast motion, strong.

II. Now, two coils sharing a core

A transformer is the device that turns this rule into a tool.

Wrap two coils around the same iron core. Push alternating current through the first one — call it the primary. Because the current alternates, the magnetic field inside the core is constantly reversing. That reversing field threads the second coil too — the secondary. By Faraday's rule, a changing field through the secondary creates a voltage in the secondary.

Whatever voltage you put on the primary appears on the secondary, scaled by the ratio of turns:

Vs ⁄ Vp = Ns ⁄ Np eq. 2 · turns ratio

More turns on the secondary means the device steps up the voltage; fewer means it steps down. That's why the brick on your laptop charger is heavy — it is, almost literally, a small transformer pressing 120 V down to 20.

III. Why the iron core matters

A funnel for flux.

— writing this section as we speak. The core is not the source of the magnetism; it is the path the field prefers. Without it, most of the primary's flux would scatter through air, miss the secondary entirely, and the device would barely transmit any voltage at all…

Definition
Magnetic flux, Φ
A measure of how much magnetic field passes through a loop. Larger field, larger area, more flux. Units: weber (Wb).
Fig. 2 · The transformer in cross-section

Two coils. Same flux. Whatever happens in one, happens in the other — almost.

Why "almost"? Real transformers leak. A tiny portion of flux escapes the core and never threads the secondary. Engineers call this leakage inductance; it's the difference between 90% and 99.5% efficiency.
Definition
Electromotive force, ℰ
The voltage that an induced field is capable of driving around a circuit. Confusingly named — it is a voltage, not a force. Units: volts.
